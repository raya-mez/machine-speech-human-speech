For more than a century, the Congressional Baseball Game has brought together lawmakers from both parties in a rare display of bipartisan camaraderie. First played in 1909, the annual contest features members of the U.S. House and Senate competing in a friendly rivalry that blends America’s pastime with public service.

The inaugural game was organized by Representative John Tener of Pennsylvania, a former professional baseball player. Though early matchups were informal and occasionally interrupted by world events, the tradition endured, becoming a beloved fixture of Washington life. By the mid-20th century, the game had grown in popularity, drawing thousands of spectators and raising funds for local charities.

Over the decades, Republicans and Democrats have formed their own teams, practicing for weeks before facing off in a spirited contest. The event has evolved into a major charitable fundraiser, generating millions of dollars for organizations such as the Boys and Girls Clubs of Greater Washington, the Washington Literacy Center, and the U.S. Capitol Police Memorial Fund.

Despite moments of national tension, the game has symbolized unity and resilience. Following the 2017 shooting at a practice session, lawmakers returned to the field in a powerful show of solidarity. Today, the Congressional Baseball Game stands as a testament to tradition, teamwork, and the enduring appeal of baseball.
