Scientists have identified 91 previously unknown volcanoes buried deep beneath the Antarctic Ice Sheet, dramatically reshaping understanding of the frozen continent’s geology. The discoveries, made using ice-penetrating radar and satellite data, reveal a vast volcanic province stretching across West Antarctica, with peaks ranging from 100 meters to nearly 4,000 meters in height.

Researchers say the hidden chain rivals some of the world’s most active volcanic regions in scale, though it remains unclear how many of the volcanoes are dormant or potentially active. Most are concealed beneath up to two kilometers of ice, making direct observation impossible without advanced imaging technology.

The findings raise important questions about the stability of the Antarctic Ice Sheet. Scientists warn that geothermal heat from volcanic activity could accelerate melting from below, potentially influencing global sea level rise. However, experts caution that more research is needed to determine whether any of the volcanoes pose an immediate threat.

The discovery also offers new insight into Antarctica’s tectonic history, suggesting the region may be more geologically dynamic than previously believed. Researchers plan further surveys to map the area in greater detail and assess how subglacial volcanic systems interact with the ice above.
