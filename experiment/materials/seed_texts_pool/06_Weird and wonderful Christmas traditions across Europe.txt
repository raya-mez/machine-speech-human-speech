From roller-skating churchgoers in Venezuela to shoe-throwing children in Germany, Europe bursts into life each December with traditions that are as quirky as they are cherished. In Catalonia, Spain, families place a smiling log called Caga Tió in their homes and “feed” it for weeks before Christmas. On Christmas Eve, children beat the log with sticks while singing, urging it to deliver sweets and small gifts.

In Austria and parts of southern Germany, the festive season has a darker edge. Men dressed as horned Krampus creatures parade through town squares, rattling chains and playfully chasing spectators, reviving a centuries-old Alpine legend meant to frighten naughty children.

Over in Ukraine, Christmas trees shimmer with artificial spider webs, inspired by a folktale in which spiders spun silver threads to help a poor family celebrate. Meanwhile in Norway, households hide their brooms on Christmas Eve to thwart mischievous spirits said to roam the night sky.

These unusual customs highlight Europe’s rich patchwork of folklore, faith and fun—reminding visitors that the magic of Christmas often lies in the delightfully unexpected. From Italy’s gift-bearing witch La Befana to the Czech custom of shoe-tossing to predict marriage, the continent embraces joyful eccentricity every festive winter season.
