The rise of Russia’s oligarchs is rooted in the turbulent transition from a state-controlled economy to capitalism in the early 1990s. When the Soviet Union collapsed, vast state assets—from oil and gas fields to metals plants and media companies—were rapidly privatized. In theory, privatization was meant to distribute ownership broadly among citizens. In practice, a small group of well-connected businessmen acquired major enterprises at a fraction of their value.

A key moment was the “loans-for-shares” program of the mid-1990s. Facing fiscal crisis, the government auctioned stakes in strategic companies in exchange for short-term loans. Insiders with political connections often won these auctions, consolidating control over lucrative natural resource industries. Weak regulatory institutions and an underdeveloped legal system allowed wealth to concentrate quickly.

Russia’s economy, heavily dependent on commodities such as oil, gas, and metals, further amplified fortunes as global prices rose in the 2000s. Close ties between business leaders and political power structures became a defining feature of the system.

Over time, the state reasserted control over key sectors, reshaping the balance between private wealth and government authority. Yet the legacy of rapid privatization endures. The concentration of assets in a handful of powerful figures remains a defining characteristic of Russia’s post-Soviet economic landscape.
