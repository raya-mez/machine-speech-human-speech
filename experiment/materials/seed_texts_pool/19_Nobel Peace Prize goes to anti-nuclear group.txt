The Nobel Peace Prize was awarded today to the International Campaign for Nuclear Abolition, honoring its decades-long effort to eliminate atomic weapons and reduce the risk of catastrophic war. The Norwegian Nobel Committee praised the coalition of grassroots activists, scientists, and survivors of nuclear testing for building global momentum behind the Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons. In its citation, the committee said the group had "reframed the debate on nuclear deterrence by highlighting the humanitarian consequences of any use."

Accepting the award in Oslo, executive director Maria Santos called the prize "a tribute to ordinary people who refuse to live under the shadow of annihilation." She urged nuclear-armed states to join disarmament talks and invest instead in health, climate, and education. Leaders from several countries welcomed the decision, while officials in nuclear powers cautioned that deterrence remains essential to national security.

Analysts said the prize could intensify diplomatic pressure ahead of next year's nonproliferation review conference, where divisions between nuclear and non-nuclear states are expected. Founded in 2007, the campaign now includes more than 600 partner organizations worldwide and has focused on survivor testimony and youth activism. Committee chair Anne Lunde said the honor recognizes "persistent moral leadership."
