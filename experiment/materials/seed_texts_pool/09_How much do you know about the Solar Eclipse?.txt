Skywatchers around the world are preparing for one of nature’s most breathtaking events: a solar eclipse. But how much do you really know about this celestial phenomenon?

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes between the Earth and the Sun, temporarily blocking some or all of the Sun’s light. There are three main types: total, partial, and annular. During a total solar eclipse, the Moon completely covers the Sun, turning day into twilight for a few dramatic minutes. In a partial eclipse, only part of the Sun is obscured. An annular eclipse happens when the Moon is too far from Earth to fully cover the Sun, leaving a bright “ring of fire” visible in the sky.

Solar eclipses do not happen every month because the Moon’s orbit is slightly tilted relative to Earth’s orbit around the Sun. When conditions align perfectly, the spectacle can be seen from specific regions of the world.

Experts warn that looking directly at a solar eclipse without proper eye protection can cause serious eye damage. Special eclipse glasses or indirect viewing methods are essential.

Beyond their beauty, solar eclipses help scientists study the Sun’s outer atmosphere, known as the corona, offering valuable insights into solar activity and space weather.
